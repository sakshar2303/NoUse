Electron tunneling is the fundamental mechanism underlying electron transfer in the mitochondrial electron transport chain (ETC), where Complexes I through IV sequentially transfer electrons from NADH and FADH₂ to molecular oxygen, coupling this process to the proton-motive force that drives ATP synthesis. Electrons tunnel quantum mechanically between redox cofactors—iron-sulfur clusters, flavin mononucleotide, heme groups, and copper centers—separated by distances up to approximately 14 Å, which represents the maximum distance permitted by the exponential decay of the tunneling matrix element. The Moser-Dutton ruler quantifies this relationship: the electron transfer rate decreases by approximately one order of magnitude for every 1.7 Å increase in edge-to-edge distance, and protein packing density modulates the effective tunneling barrier. Marcus theory of electron transfer provides the theoretical framework, describing the rate as dependent on three parameters: the electronic coupling (tunneling matrix element), the driving force (ΔG°), and the reorganization energy λ, which reflects the cost of rearranging the nuclear framework between reactant and product states. In Complex I (NADH:ubiquinone oxidoreductase), electrons tunnel through seven iron-sulfur clusters spanning ~95 Å, with each step maintained below the 14 Å limit. Ubiquinone serves as a mobile electron carrier that shuttles electrons from Complexes I and II to Complex III within the inner mitochondrial membrane. Cytochrome c transfers electrons from Complex III to Complex IV (cytochrome c oxidase), where four electrons reduce oxygen to water. The proton-motive force generated by Complexes I, III, and IV drives ATP synthase, coupling quantum tunneling directly to cellular energy production.
