The persistence of quantum effects in warm, wet biological environments presents a fundamental puzzle, since decoherence—the loss of quantum superposition through environmental interaction—typically occurs on femtosecond to picosecond timescales in condensed-phase systems at physiological temperatures. Environment-assisted quantum transport (ENAQT), developed by Huelga, Plenio, and collaborators, resolves this paradox by demonstrating that moderate environmental noise enhances rather than destroys quantum transport efficiency. In the Huelga-Plenio model, the phonon bath of the protein introduces dephasing noise that prevents destructive interference and Anderson localization, which facilitates energy transfer via noise-assisted transport. The structured spectral density of protein vibrational modes is critical: rather than acting as a featureless thermal reservoir, the protein provides specific frequencies resonant with electronic energy gaps, which sustains coherent dynamics within the relevant transfer timescale. Non-Markovian dynamics—in which the environment retains memory of past interactions—further support quantum effect persistence, because the structured bath feeds information back into the quantum system, which partially reverses decoherence. The Caldeira-Leggett model describes the coupling between a quantum biological subsystem and its thermal environment through a continuum of harmonic oscillators. The quantum Zeno effect, in which frequent measurement slows quantum state evolution, may stabilize intermediate states during energy or electron transfer. An optimal dephasing rate maximizes transport efficiency—too little noise causes localization while too much noise produces classical diffusion, both of which reduce efficiency relative to the ENAQT regime. This framework explains how photosynthetic complexes, enzyme active sites, and radical pairs exploit quantum effects despite thermal noise.
