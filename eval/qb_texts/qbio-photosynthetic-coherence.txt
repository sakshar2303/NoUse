Quantum coherence in photosynthetic light harvesting was dramatically demonstrated in the Fenna-Matthews-Olson (FMO) complex of the green sulfur bacterium Chlorobaculum tepidum, where excitonic energy transfer achieves efficiencies exceeding 95%. The FMO complex contains seven bacteriochlorophyll a (BChl a) chromophores arranged within a protein scaffold, and these chromophores are electronically coupled such that absorbed photon energy delocalizes across multiple pigments as exciton states. In 2007, Fleming and colleagues used two-dimensional (2D) electronic spectroscopy to detect long-lived quantum beating signals in FMO at cryogenic temperatures (77 K), providing direct evidence that quantum coherence persists during energy transfer on timescales of hundreds of femtoseconds. These quantum beating signals arise from superpositions of excitonic states, which enables the exciton to sample multiple energy transfer pathways simultaneously rather than following a single classical hopping route. This wavelike exploration of the energy landscape is hypothesized to enhance transfer efficiency by avoiding kinetic traps. Subsequent experiments by Engel, Panitchayangkoon, and others extended observations to physiological temperatures (~300 K), though the persistence and functional relevance of room temperature coherence remains actively debated. Decoherence—the destruction of quantum superpositions by environmental interactions—occurs on femtosecond timescales in the warm, wet protein environment, yet the structured phonon bath of the protein may paradoxically sustain coherence by tuning vibrational modes to match electronic energy gaps. The protein environment thus protects coherence rather than merely destroying it, a finding that challenges the assumption that biological systems are too noisy for quantum effects. Energy transfer from FMO to the reaction center drives charge separation, which initiates the photosynthetic electron transport chain, linking quantum coherence directly to the organism's metabolic function.
