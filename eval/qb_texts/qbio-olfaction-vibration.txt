The vibrational theory of olfaction, proposed by Luca Turin in 1996, posits that odorant discrimination depends on the vibrational frequencies of molecules rather than solely on their shape, challenging the dominant lock-and-key shape theory. In Turin's model, odorant receptors—which belong to the G-protein-coupled receptor (GPCR) superfamily—function as biological spectroscopes that detect molecular vibrations via inelastic electron tunneling spectroscopy (IETS). When an odorant binds within the receptor's binding pocket, an electron tunnels from a donor to an acceptor site only if the odorant's vibrational mode matches the energy gap between these states, which enables frequency-selective detection. This phonon-assisted tunneling process transfers one quantum of vibrational energy from the odorant to the tunneling electron, which activates the receptor and triggers downstream G-protein signal transduction. Deuterium isotope experiments provide critical tests: replacing hydrogen with deuterium alters vibrational frequencies (due to doubled atomic mass) without changing molecular shape. Studies in Drosophila melanogaster demonstrated that flies can distinguish acetophenone from deuterated acetophenone, which supports the prediction that different vibrational spectra produce different odor percepts. The Brookes-Horsfield model formalizes phonon-assisted tunneling by incorporating spectral density of the protein environment and the coupling strength between odorant vibration and the electron transfer coordinate. Shape theory adequately explains most olfactory discrimination through molecular recognition, and some replication attempts have yielded conflicting results. Nevertheless, the vibrational theory expands quantum biology into chemosensory systems.
