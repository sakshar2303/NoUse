The radical pair mechanism provides the leading quantum mechanical explanation for avian magnetoreception, enabling migratory birds such as the European robin (Erithacus rubecula) to detect Earth's magnetic field (~50 μT). Klaus Schulten first proposed in 1978 that geomagnetic navigation depends on magnetically sensitive chemical reactions, and this hypothesis was formalized into the radical pair model. Cryptochrome proteins, particularly CRY1 and CRY4, located in the retina of migratory birds serve as candidate magnetoreceptor molecules. Blue light activates the flavin adenine dinucleotide (FAD) cofactor within cryptochrome, which drives sequential electron transfer along a chain of tryptophan residues (Trp triad), generating a spin-correlated radical pair. In this pair, two unpaired electrons exist in a quantum superposition of singlet and triplet spin states. Hyperfine interactions between electron spins and nearby nuclear spins modulate the singlet-triplet interconversion rate, and the external magnetic field direction modulates this interconversion anisotropically. The Ritz model predicts that this anisotropy produces a direction-dependent signal that the bird perceives as a visual pattern, effectively providing a magnetic compass. Spin coherence must persist for at least one microsecond, which requires the protein environment to shield the radical pair from rapid decoherence. Radiofrequency electromagnetic fields in the low-MHz range disrupt magnetic orientation in European robins, which supports the radical pair mechanism because such fields resonantly disturb singlet-triplet dynamics. CRY4 expression increases during migratory seasons, strengthening its association with magnetoreception.
