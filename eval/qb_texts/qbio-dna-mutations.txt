Per-Olov Löwdin proposed in 1963 that quantum mechanical proton tunneling within DNA base pairs causes spontaneous point mutations by inducing tautomeric shifts in nucleotide bases, providing a quantum origin for a fraction of replication errors. In standard Watson-Crick base pairing, guanine pairs with cytosine (G–C) through three hydrogen bonds and adenine pairs with thymine (A–T) through two hydrogen bonds. Löwdin's hypothesis states that a proton in a hydrogen bond can tunnel through the double-well potential energy barrier separating the normal tautomer from a rare tautomer, which shifts the proton to the opposite nitrogen or oxygen atom. This tautomeric shift alters the hydrogen bonding pattern, which causes the rare tautomer to mispair during DNA replication: the rare enol tautomer of guanine pairs with thymine instead of cytosine, producing a G·C → A·T transition mutation. The tunneling probability depends on barrier width and height, which differ between G–C and A–T base pairs; the triple hydrogen bond network of G–C generates a different tunneling landscape than A–T, which produces base-pair-specific mutation rates. Thermal fluctuations at physiological temperature (310 K) rapidly dephase quantum superpositions, which threatens to suppress coherent tunneling. However, the proton transfer distance in DNA hydrogen bonds is extremely short (~0.6–0.8 Å), which enables tunneling to complete faster than decoherence destroys the quantum state. Computational studies using density functional theory and path integral molecular dynamics support the existence of metastable rare tautomers with lifetimes sufficient to persist through the replication fork. The spontaneous mutation rate (~10⁻⁸ to 10⁻¹⁰ per base pair per replication) is partially attributable to this tunneling process, though enzymatic proofreading and mismatch repair systems correct the majority of tunneling-induced errors.
