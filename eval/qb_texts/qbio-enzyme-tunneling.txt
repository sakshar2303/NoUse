Quantum tunneling plays a fundamental role in enzyme catalysis, enabling hydrogen and proton transfer reactions that would be prohibitively slow under classical transition state theory alone. In alcohol dehydrogenase (ADH), the transfer of a hydride ion from substrate to the NAD+ cofactor proceeds via quantum tunneling through the activation energy barrier rather than over it, which accelerates the reaction rate by several orders of magnitude. Kinetic isotope effects (KIE) provide the primary experimental evidence for tunneling: replacing hydrogen with deuterium increases the effective mass of the tunneling particle, which reduces the tunneling probability and slows the reaction. Anomalously large KIE values—exceeding the semiclassical limit of approximately 7—have been observed in aromatic amine dehydrogenase (AADH), strongly implicating tunneling as the dominant transfer mechanism. Marcus-like models describe this process by coupling the tunneling matrix element to a Marcus-type free energy framework, where the tunneling correction factor quantifies the deviation from purely classical over-the-barrier kinetics. Protein conformational sampling modulates tunneling efficiency by transiently compressing the donor–acceptor distance, thereby narrowing the potential energy barrier width. These transient compressions are driven by promoting vibrations—specific protein motions that reduce the tunneling distance from ~0.9 Å to shorter values, which exponentially increases tunneling probability. Temperature-dependent KIE studies reveal that promoting vibrations couple to the chemical coordinate, linking protein dynamics to the quantum mechanical event. The protein scaffold thus acts as a finely tuned environment that optimizes barrier width and shape, enabling tunneling to dominate at physiological temperatures. This challenges the classical view that enzymes function solely by stabilizing the transition state, demonstrating instead that quantum mechanical effects are integral to biological catalysis.
